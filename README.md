### ‚ú®Prepare Phase

#### ‚û§Data being used:

#### ‚û§Data Summary:
The datasets was published to Kaggle under the CC0: Public Domain Creative Common License. Allowing for the datasets to be copied, modified and distributed without asking for permission.

According to its information, the datasets are generated by respondents to a distributed survey via Amazon Mechanical Turk between 03.12.2016-05.12.2016.  Thirty FitBit users consented to submit their personal tracker data, including minute-level output for physical activity, heart rate, and sleep monitoring. Additionally, it was also stated that the difference in the output can be attributed to the use of different Fitbit tracker models and the user‚Äôs own ways of tracking their activities.

#### ‚û§Data Organization:

There are eighteen datasets from the **[FitBit Fitness Tracker Data](https://www.kaggle.com/datasets/arashnic/fitbit)** and are downloaded in .csv file and with long and wide formats. Data is long because each subject has multiple rows of data, representing a one-time point.

I was able to verify that 33 user has their own unique ID and time length of analysis is 31 days.

#### ‚û§Data Integrity and Limitations:

The datasets were collected in 2016, which makes them outdated. Moreover, after verifying the data, it only covered a 31-day period (04-12-2016 to 05-12-2016), which is a relatively short timeframe. 

Also, the sample size of 33 may lead to sample bias and limits the ability to make recommendations for a larger population. Furthermore, the lack of demographic information makes it difficult to accurately identify the target audience and provide relevant marketing recommendations. This information would have been helpful for Bellabeat, considering that their primary audience is women and individuals who menstruate.

Getting a more insightful analysis can be relatively small due to various limitations of the datasets.

Next I wanted to breakdown the users into three criteria based on how often they wear their tracker. I categorized the users as follows:

#### ‚û§User Type per Activity Level:

Since there is no demographic variables to distinct the users from our datasets, I classify the users by their daily steps as their level of activity.

We can classify the users as follows:

- **Sedentary** - Less than 5000 steps a day.
- **Lightly active** - Between 5000 and 7499 steps a day.
- **Fairly active** - Between 7500 and 9999 steps a day.
- **Very active** - More than 10000 steps a day.

In general, the recommended steps daily is 10,000 for adults. But this still depends on the person‚Äôs age and fitness level to achieve health benefits according to these articles:

- [How many steps should people take per day?](https://www.medicalnewstoday.com/articles/how-many-steps-should-you-take-a-day)
- [Counting Your Steps](https://www.10000steps.org.au/articles/healthy-lifestyles/counting-steps/)


```SQL
SELECT
    Id,
    AVG(StepTotal) avg_daily_steps,
    CASE
        WHEN AVG(StepTotal) <=5000 THEN 'Inactive user'
        WHEN AVG(StepTotal) <=7499 THEN 'Low active user'
        WHEN AVG(StepTotal) <=9999 THEN 'Average active user'
        WHEN AVG(StepTotal) >=10000 THEN 'Very active user'
        END AS user_type
FROM
    bellabeat_capstone.dbo.daily_steps 
    GROUP BY Id;
```
